# Thinking

Good decisions do not come from being smart. They come from refusing to let an idea pass without a fight, then committing once it survives.

These four skills are the discipline for that. One sets the default posture: tell me the truth, not what I want to hear. Two stress-test an idea from different angles. One turns the surviving idea into a decision you can act on and defend. Used together, they move you from conviction to scrutiny to commitment without skipping the middle step, which is the step most people skip.

## How They Work Together

[Anti-Sycophancy](./anti-sycophancy/SKILL.md) is the baseline, not a separate exercise. It reframes anything you bring as a neutral question before answering, so the response evaluates the idea instead of flattering the person who had it. Run it under everything else. When you catch yourself fishing for a "sounds good," that is exactly when it earns its place.

[Cynical Geoff](./cynical-geoff/SKILL.md) is the adversary. It steel-mans your argument first, then tries to kill it: hidden assumptions, second-order consequences, the comfortable narrative that feels true and isn't. Bring an idea here when you want it genuinely stress-tested, not validated.

[Executive Review Board](./executive-review-board/SKILL.md) widens the lens. It puts your idea in front of six operators who each think differently, so you hear what Bezos finds missing, what Jobs finds inelegant, and what Buffett finds financially illogical before someone else does. Use it when the blind spot is a perspective you don't have.

[Decision Brief](./decision-brief/SKILL.md) is the constructive counterpart. Where the other three pull an idea apart, this one puts the surviving pieces back together into a one-page memo: options, tradeoffs, a recommendation, and the reasoning. It is what turns analysis into a call you can act on and a record of why you made it.

The natural sequence is scrutiny then commitment: stress-test with Cynical Geoff or the Review Board, then write the Decision Brief. Anti-Sycophancy runs the whole time.

## The Skills

| Skill | Trigger | Primary Function | Core Discipline |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Anti-Sycophancy](./anti-sycophancy/SKILL.md) | Default / on demand | Reframe affirmation-seeking input into neutral evaluation | Truth over agreement |
| [Cynical Geoff](./cynical-geoff/SKILL.md) | On demand | Steel-man, then disconfirm an idea | Disconfirmation |
| [Executive Review Board](./executive-review-board/SKILL.md) | On demand | Six-perspective critique with a consolidated verdict | Perspective diversity |
| [Decision Brief](./decision-brief/SKILL.md) | After analysis | One-page memo: options, tradeoffs, recommendation | Commitment with a record |

Each skill follows the same self-documenting format: frontmatter (name, trigger, typed inputs, tools), an overview, a workflow, an output spec, and lessons learned where the skill calls for them.

## Philosophy

Most bad calls are not failures of intelligence. They are failures of nerve, where the analysis stopped the moment it returned the answer the person already wanted. An idea that has only been confirmed has not been tested. A decision made without naming its tradeoffs is a guess wearing a suit.

These skills favor disconfirmation over reassurance, specifics over hedges, and one page over ten. They name the mechanism of a flaw, not a vague worry. They state the strongest version of the opposing case, then say what they actually think. And they end where thinking is supposed to end: with a decision and the reasoning that backs it, not with more options to admire.

The test is simple. Did the idea get harder to defend, and did it survive anyway? That is the only kind of conviction worth acting on.

## Recommended Next Skills

The suite covers posture, stress-testing, perspective, and the decision itself. The honest gap is what happens after the call:

**Pre-Mortem** (before committing real resources). Assume the decision failed, then work backward to the most likely causes while there is still time to prevent them. Cynical Geoff runs a version of this inside its workflow, but a standalone Pre-Mortem on a locked Decision Brief is worth its own pass.

**Decision Log** (after the fact). Record the call, the reasoning, and the assumptions it rested on, then revisit when the outcome lands. This is how judgment compounds: most people never check whether the decision was right, only whether it felt right at the time.

## Connection to the Work

Leaders are paid to make calls under uncertainty and own them. The scarce skill is not having opinions. It is holding your own idea to the standard you would hold a rival's, then committing once it clears the bar. These skills are the mechanism for doing that on purpose instead of by luck.
